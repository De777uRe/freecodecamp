Clive Staples "C.S." Lewis

Professor, Theologian, and Author of The Chronicles of Narnia

Here's a timeline of C.S. Lewis's life:

  • 1898 - Born in Belfast, Ireland.
  • 1905 - Moves into "Little Lea", the family home of his childhood, in the Strandtown area of East Belfast.
  • 1908 - Mother dies from cancer, and father sends him to live and study in Wynyard School in Watford, Hertfordshire where his brother had enrolled 3 years prior. The school is closed not long afterward due to a lack of pupils, and the headmaster Robert "Oldie" Capron was committed to a psychiatric hospital. Attends Campbell College in East Belfast, but leaves after a few months due to respiratory problems. Sent to the health-resort town of Malvern, Worcestershire, where he attends the preparatory school Cherbourg House. During this time abandons his childhood Christian faith, becoming interested in mythology and the occult.
  • 1913 - Enrolls in Malvern College. Finds the school socially competitive.
  • 1916 - Awarded a scholarship at University College, Oxford.
  • 1917 - Enters Oxford in the summer term. Joins the Officers' Training Corps at the university as his "most promising route into the army". Drafted into a Cadet Battalion for training. After training, commissioned into the Third Battalion of the Somerset Light Infantry of the British Army as a Second Lieutenant. Within months of entering Oxford, shipped into France to fight in the First World War. On his 19th birthday, arrives at the front line in the Somme Valley in France and experiences trench warfare for the first time.
  • 1918 - Wounded and two colleagues are killed by a British shell falling short of its target. Suffers from depression and homesickness during convalescence and upon recovery is assigned to duty in Andover, England. Demobilised in December and soon restarts studies. His friend Edward Courtnay Francis "Paddy" Moore, whom he was roommates with during cadet training is killed in action. The two had promised each other that if either died during the war, the survivor would take care of both of their families. He will go on to keep this promise and live with Paddy's mother Janie King Moore until she is hospitalized in the late 1940s. Will later cite in a letter his experience of the horror of war, along with the loss of his mother and unhappiness in school as the bases of his pessimism and atheism.
  • 1920 - After returning to Oxford University, receives a First in Honour Moderations (Greek and Latin literature)
  • 1921 - Meets W. B. Yeats, a famous Irish poet, twice. Is surprised to find his English peers indifferent to Yeats and the Celtic Revival movement, writing: "I am often surprised to find how utterly ignored Yeats is among the men I have met: perhaps his appeal is purely Irish - if so, then thank the gods that I am Irish.
  • 1922 - Receives a First in Greats (Philosophy and Ancient History).
  • 1923 - Receives a First in English resulting in winning a triple first, the highest honors in 3 areas of study.
  • 1924 - Becomes philosophy tutor at University College.
  • 1925 - Elected a Fellow and Tutor in English Literature at Magdalen College where he will serve for 29 years.
  • 1926 - Meets Oxford colleague and Christian friend J. R. R. Tolkien.
  • 1929 - Converts to theism.
  • 1931 - Converts to Christianity.
  • 1933 - Publishes his first novel after becoming a Christian: The Pilgrim's Regress which depicts his experience with Christianity in the style of John Bunyan's The Pilgrim's Progress.
  • 1935 - Along with his brother Warren, donates a stained glass window in memeory of their parents to their childhood church of St. Mark's, Dundela, Belfast.
  • 1939 - Begins taking child evacuees from London and other cities into The Kilns after the outbreak of the Second World War.
  • 1941 - Begins speaking on religious programs broadcast by the BBC from London while the city was under periodic air raids. Regarding these broadcasts, Air Chief Marshal Sir Donald Hardman wrote: "The war, the whole of life, everything tended to seem pointless. We needed, many of us, a key to the meaning of the universe. Lewis provided just that."
  • 1942 - Becomes the first President of the Oxford Socratic Club.
  • 1943 - Presents a series of lectures at Durham University which would later more be presented more formally in The Abolition of Man.
  • 1949 - Begins work on The Chronicles of Narnia.
  • 1951 - Named on the last list of honours by George VI as a Commander of the Order of the British Empire but declined so as to avoid association with any political issues.
  • 1954 - Appointed to Cambridge University, accepting the newly founded chair of Mediaeval and Renaissance Literature. Elected a fellow of Magdalene College.
  • 1956 - Enters into a civil marriage with Joy Davidman Gresham (an American writer) so she could continue to live in the UK.
  • 1957 - Joins Joy in a Christian marriage performed by a friend, Rev. Peter Bide, at her bed in the Churchill Hospital after she was diagnosed with terminal bone cancer.
  • 1958 - Spends his honeymoon at the Old Inn, Crawfordsburn in Northern Ireland, which he calls "my Irish life".
  • 1960 - After Joy's cancer recurs after remission, she tragically dies which becomes the subject of A Grief Observed.
  • 1961 - Begins suffering from nephritis, which results in blood poisoning and him missing the autumn term at Cambridge.
  • 1962 - Health gradually improves and allows a return to Cambridge.
  • 1963 - Suffers a heart attack and lapses into a coma. After discharge, returns to the Kilns unable to return to work. Resigns from post at Cambridge. Is diagnosed with end-stage kidney failure at the end of the year and dies shortly after, exactly one week before his 65th birthday.

"To love at all is to be vulnerable. Love anything and your heart will be wrung and possibly broken. If you want to make sure of keeping it intact you must give it to no one, not even an animal. Wrap it carefully round with hobbies and little luxuries; avoid all engtanglements. Lock it up safe in the casket or coffin of your selfishness. But in that casket, safe, dark, motionless, airless, it will change. It will not be broken; it will become unbreakable, impenetrable, irredeemable. To love is to be vulnerable." -- C.S. Lewis
